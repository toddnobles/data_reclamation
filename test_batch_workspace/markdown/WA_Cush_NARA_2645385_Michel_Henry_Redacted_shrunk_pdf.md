Dear Mr. Peel;

Louise Michel, the mother of Henry Michel, has called at this office and shown me a letter dated January 13, from Peter Moses to his father, Joe Moses, alleging that Henry Michel is sick and wants to go home. Mrs. Michel has also shown me a letter from Henry dated January 21, addressed to his sister, Susanne Michel, stating that he is just about to enter the hospital. He states, "I don't think Morgan will let me go home if I do ask him."

Mrs. Michel is naturally concerned as to Henry, and I have assured her that we have received no reports here that he is sick, and that I had no reason to believe that there was anything serious ailing him.

I wish that you would have Henry write to his mother assuring her as to his condition and I believe that a letter from you to her would quiet her anxiety.

I regret the evident inclination on Peter's part to incite dissatisfaction at the school.

Very truly yours,

Fred C. Morgan
Superintendent

Mrs. Louise Michel
c/o Colville Indian Agency

Dear Madam:

I have received a request to write you in regard to your boy, Henry. It seems that he has been writing you that he is sick and wants to come home. I have to advise that, so far as I can ascertain, he had one or two little colds during the month of January and was sent to the school hospital for treatment, as is done in all cases when a pupil has the least kind of illness. You probably know that we maintain a hospital in connection with our school here and it is just a few rods from the several dormitories and when a pupil becomes the least bit ill, we send him, or her, to the hospital for treatment. Henry has not been seriously ill at any time. If he had been, we would have notified you, but we think it unnecessary to notify parents when their child happens to have a little cold or cough.

As far as I can learn, Henry is getting along nicely in school and is learning something every day which is bound to benefit him throughout his life time. Should he go home now, he probably would never enter school again and would no doubt go thru life and will be sorry that he did not stay in school as long as he possibly could. I would be very glad to see him finish his term here this year and if this school is permanently closed in June to enter the Indian school at Chemawa, Oregon, next fall.

Trusting that this will ease your conscience as to your son's welfare, I am

Yours very truly,

Special Agent in Charge.
Cushman Indian School  
Tacoma, Wash., June 12, 1919

My dear Mr. Michell:

I have been informed that you are objecting to your son's accompanying our Band on its summer trip. I am sure your objections are based on a misunderstanding of the purpose of this trip and the uncertainty as to whether your son will be properly cared for during this trip.

Mr. Fred W. Freeman is an Indian employee who has been detailed to accompany the Band for the sole purpose of seeing that your son, and the other boys, receive careful attention and care. Mr. Freeman's son, Freddy, who is about ten years old, is a member of the Band and will accompany the Band, and I assure you that Mr. Freeman will take just as good care of your son as he will of his own. The boys will be looked after as carefully as if they were at home and will not be allowed to wander about unless accompanied by Mr. Freeman. They will be under his eyes and in his care every moment of the time they are away from this school, until they return and, when they do return, they will be given permission to visit home before school, so that their parents will see that they have been well taken care of and will hear from them all about the wonderful things they have seen and heard during their trip.

If the boys were to pay for this trip, it would cost them not less than $1,000.00 to make it. They will visit sixty-eight cities and will play before the President of the United States. It is a wonderful opportunity and I feel sure you will not be so selfish as to rob your boy of the chance to go with his fellow school-mates of the Band.

Very sincerely yours,

Superintendent.

EXTRACTS FROM SCHOOL RULES.

If the facilities of a school are not needed as provided in the above paragraph, children showing any Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, and who if debarred from Government schools could not obtain an education, may be enrolled in the day and boarding schools of their reservation; the children, however, may not be transferred to nonreservation schools without special permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

Before enrolling a pupil superintendents must satisfy themselves whether he has left any other school without permission and whether he has been expelled for cause from any other school. A pupil who has been expelled for cause from any Indian school may be reenrolled in any Indian school only with permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Attention is invited to Circulars Nos. 1039 and 1044, which must be observed.

"The Secretary of the Interior may in his discretion withhold rations, clothing, and other annuities from Indian parents or guardians who refuse or neglect to send or keep their children of proper school age in some school during a reasonable portion of each year."
