INDORSEMENT.

A child showing one-sixteenth or less Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, Indian fashion, who, if debarred from the Government schools, could not obtain an education, may be permitted in the reservation day and boarding schools, but it is preferable that it be not transferred to a nonreservation school, without special permission from the Office. Children showing one-eighth or less Indian blood, whose parents do not live on an Indian reservation, whose home is among white people where there are churches and schools, who are presumed to have adopted the white man's manners and customs, and are to all intents and purposes white people, are debarred from enrollment in the Government nonreservation and reservation schools. Superintendents, in all cases where doubt exists as to the degree of Indian blood of a child proposed for transfer, should fully satisfy themselves of the facts by affidavits from reliable persons, which affidavits must be kept on file at the school.

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both Superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and Superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

A pupil dismissed from school for cause must not be enrolled in any other school without the permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Full facts must be submitted with each request.

Dear Mr. Brown:

Mrs. Peterson of Seattle was in to see me last Saturday about your daughter and Frank Mark's daughter returning home. TO ALL MEMBERS OF THE CUSHION STUDENT ASSOCIATION

Dear Friends:

Nearly two years have passed since our last Reunion and Conference and I assure you it was with the keenest pleasure and expectation that I received word from Mr. Hammond, present Superintendent of Cushion, that I may again, in the name of the Association and of the School Faculty, invite you to the Seventh Annual Reunion and Conference of the Former Students of the Cushion School, to be held at the school June 17, 1918.

The Reunion this year should be an event of unusual significance. The two years just past have seen many changes, both in the management and student body of the school. We should feel the necessity of our getting acquainted with the pupils and giving them the encouragement of our interests and support. We should get familiar with the advanced methods of the school that we may see at first hand the possibilities there are for the education of our children. We should keep in close touch with the school that our criticism may be always just and constructive; that our support may be genuine and effective.

The program for the day includes three distinct features that we should come prepared to make the most of.

Let us make the selection of our officers and the new organization of our Association a matter of first importance.

Come prepared to take part in the afternoon conference and to say a word at the evening patriotic meeting. Let us put the best there is in us into the Association and work to make the Association an effective force in the education and uplift of our race. Make your presence in some way a contribution to the interest of your old school. I can assure you it will be a pleasure you will long remember.

Very cordially yours,
V. H. Johnson, President.
