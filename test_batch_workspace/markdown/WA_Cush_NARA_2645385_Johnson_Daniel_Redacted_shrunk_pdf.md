Dear Mr. Johnson:

Daniel Franklin Johnson was in school at the time the receipt was mailed you October first.

During the summer months Daniel was given permission with other large boys to work in the Puyallup Valley to earn spending money. He returned at the re-opening of school and remained in school for a week or so when he again tried to reach Alaska, but was headed off at Seattle and forced to return after a week's absence. This was his third and last attempt.

I had a long talk with him and told him that since he would not listen to me he would have to be court-martialed by the student-body. The Battalion officers did court-martial him and sentenced him to go to school and to the Industrial Departments under guard and to sleep in Jail one month. I approved this sentence but after a week I pardoned Daniel and his comrade as they both had decided that it was better to do what was right. Since then Daniel has been working hard. I gave him a five-weeks course in shoe and harness repairing, which he enjoyed and in which he did some very excellent work. He has been detailed to the Engineering Department, in which he is particularly interested. His work in this department is creditable. His instructor is taking an interest in him and if Daniel will give heed to our advice and our instruction, he will not regret the time that he spent at Cushman.

He is well and much stouter than when he came to school. I will have him write you himself if he has not done so.

Very sincerely yours,

Superintendent.

This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All the blanks must be properly filled in every case.

INDORSEMENTS.

Applicable laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

Compliance must be given to all orders and instructions promulgated in connection with the new course of study issued December 1, 1915.

The information as to public school facilities called for in the certificate of the Superintendent (third page hereof) must be given, and all superintendents are especially directed to exhaust every means to secure enrollment of Indian children in the State public schools, pursuant to instructions heretofore issued in this regard.

Dear Sir,

I received your letter of July 28, and note what you say about my son's action. I was not at all surprised to learn that he had taken French leave, and it is quite likely that he will try the same thing in the future. The letter which you say he wrote has not been received. In fact, he has not written home more than 3 months. It will be necessary for you to keep him under very strict discipline for some time, at least.

Does he take any interest in books or his work? I wish you would let me know from time to time how he is getting along.

Yours truly,  
J.L. Johnson

Tacoma, Wash., Aug. 27, 1919

Mr. T. F. Johnson
Kasaan,
Alaska

Dear Mr. Johnson:

Your son, Danile Franklin, has been improving both in conduct and application since my last letter. He was returned forcibly to school and was placed in charge of one of the employees who was instructed to keep an eye on him. I am very glad to tell you that Daniel responded to the employee's manner of treatment and really applied himself. He has been doing such good work as painter that I placed him on the payroll this last week to enable him to earn a little spending money for the fair next October. If I could only make Daniel stop chewing tobacco, it would be very much better for him.

Very truly yours,

Superintendent.

Tacoma, Wash., July 28, 1919

T. F. Johnson
Kasaan,
Alaska

My dear Mr. Johnson:

You have, by this time, received the Official Receipt for the payment of the tuition for the second quarter.

Daniel is becoming very fat, but occasionally insists that he has consumption. After the close of school, he and the McAlpin boys left without permission and picked berries in the country for several days before I found them. Daniel was punished reasonably for this misbehavior and wrote you a most heart-rending letter declaring that he was dying of consumption and begging you to send him home. He is, as I have said, becoming very stout and I hope in time he will change his attitude as you know he will benefit very much from the reasonable discipline and the excellent work of industrial instruction given him.

If you know of any more boys, fourteen years old, or older, who should be in school, I shall be glad to promise them every opportunity to make good here.

Sincerely yours,

Superintendent.

April 8, 1919.

Mr. T. F. Johnson  
Kasaan, Alaska.

Dear Sir:

Your son, David Franklin, arrived safely at Cushman. Daniel will naturally feel homesick and will probably consider a return to Alaska any time within the next six months. I shall, however, try to interest him in his work and studies so that he will become attached to the school and will strive to profit by the advantages offered him. If he leaves school without my knowledge or consent, I shall have him traced and brought back until his three year term has expired.

The Government charges parents who are able to support their children a tuition fee of $200.00 a year. We have a number of Alaskans whose parents pay their tuition at Cushman. If you are in a position to do so, I advise you to do it as it will undoubtedly exert a good influence over Daniel and encourage him to make the best of his opportunities.

Daniel arrived at the school with a bad cold and told me that he did not think he would stay long as he had consumption. I had our doctor examine him immediately and found that his lungs weren't affected in the least degree, so you need not worry if he writes you that he has consumption. It will mean that he is homesick and wishes you to take him out of school.

Very truly yours,

Superintendent
