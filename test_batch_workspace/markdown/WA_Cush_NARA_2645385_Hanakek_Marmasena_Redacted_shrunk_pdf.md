The law provides:
"That no part of this appropriation, or any other appropriation provided for herein except appropriations made pursuant to treaties, shall be used to educate children of less than one-fourth Indian blood whose parents are citizens of the United States and of the State wherein they live and where there are adequate free-school facilities provided and the facilities of the Indian schools are needed for pupils of more than one-fourth Indian blood."

EXTRACTS FROM SCHOOL RULES.

If the facilities of a school are not needed as provided in the above paragraph, children showing any Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, and who if debarred from Government schools could not obtain an education, may be enrolled in the day and boarding schools of their reservation; these children, however, may not be transferred to nonreservation schools without special permission from the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

Before enrolling a pupil superintendents must satisfy themselves whether he has left any other school without permission and whether he has been expelled for cause from any other school. A pupil who has been expelled for cause from any Indian school may be reenrolled in any Indian school only with permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Attention is invited to Circulars Nos. 1039 and 1044, which must be observed.

"The Secretary of the Interior may in his discretion withhold rations, clothing, and other annuities from Indian parents or guardians who refuse or neglect to send or keep their children of proper school age in some school during a reasonable portion of each year." (27 Stat. L., 635. See 2 Dec. Comp., 308.)

April 14, 1920.

Mr. Peel

Dear Sir,

I write to ask you if you can allow Marmasina to come to Seattle to stay with me and my family. As I know she would like to be with us. I have known her ever since childhood. Me and my daughter visited your school a short while ago. And I am sure I can get her a position with my oldest daughter.
We will take the best of care of her and be responsible for her.

If you should require any references in regards to our moral character, I can refer you to Mr. Lopp, the head of Bureau of Education of Alaska, also Mr. Goodman of the Hudson Bay Co. Mr. Lopp has known my husband for nearly 30 years, who is a pioneer of Alaska.

And answer will oblige,

Yours sincerely,
Mrs. Mary Gordon.

Tacoma, Wash., April 19, 1920

Mrs. Mary Gordon  
108 Broadway  
Seattle, Washington

Dear Madam:

Replying to your letter of the 14th instant requesting me to allow Marmasena Hanakek to stay with you and your family in Seattle during the summer, I have to advise that this meets with my approval, provided you will use your influence to get her to attend some good school next fall. She can leave here immediately after the close of school. She spoke to me about this some time ago and I told her I thought it would be all right.

Marmasena is an excellent girl and I would be glad to see her continue her training as long as possible in order that she might go back to her people and assist them to lead better and more useful lives.

I will talk the matter over with her again and let you know when to expect her.

Yours very truly,

Special Agent in Charge.

Mr. C.V. Peel, agent in charge,
Cushman Indian School
Tacoma, Washington.

Dear Sir:

In receipt of letter announcing the discontinuance of Cushman. Am very sorry but know nothing of its reasons nor needs and am glad there is a choice of places than pupils may be transferred to. Am leaving the choice of Marmasena Kanakeh's transfer to her own choice. Have just now mailed her special delivery letter to that effect including permission for her to go North to the cannery work for the summer.
I do not know the location of the Philalip, Washington school but will look it up. Do know the Chemawa, though as my early youth was spent not far from here and I well remember seeing the buildings in progress, and the happy faces of well cared for pupils later as I have passed the place.

Do everything that is best both as to advice and transfer for Marmasue for she is deserving and capable of the best.

Have written her saying I hoped it would be possible for her to have her examination before leaving that nothing would stand in her way upon her return to school next fall, but in view of the hurry and the short time I realize it may be impossible.

Sincerely yours,

(Mrs. A. L.) Frances E. Derbyshire

26 April 1920.

If the telegram I sent Mr. Hammond last year relative to Marmasue's going north is available it will enlighten and help your decision and consent to her going; also my thoughts in the charter.
