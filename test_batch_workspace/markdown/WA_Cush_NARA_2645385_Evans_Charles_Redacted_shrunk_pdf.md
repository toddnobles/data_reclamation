Dear Mr. Evans,

I thought I would write to you while I am writing to Clara. Well, how you have served still thinking of yourself. I use to think how Alice treated you. I guess all right. She got mad at you just so she can go with Henry B. That's what I heard anyway. I don't know how true it is though. I heard still—you get even if you go with someone else. And I hope you get a good—hope you get married. I sure hope she will be a nice one.
If I didn't like you I wouldn't have asked you for a sore knee evening. Why didn't you give me one for膝盖 sake. I would of pressed it and kept it. But I has I want you to take good care of the ties I gave you.

You know what I wanted to speak to you yesterday morning before you and boys went to Seattle but elders want to go all alone.

Charles are some folks going around I mean you band boys or not if not where are you folks going this summer or sometime going to Alaska?

Well as I was picking strawberries today for a while I spent picked 4 crates and 1 box of strawberry that way why well I love to cook so and I got it over here about to cook for the pickers as well it is just for my sister in law and may be her but my boys aren't picking. George Brady was at here he just left oh I don't like him I never did any way and I don't think I will either.

Well I just got one stand left. I have to get some tomorrow from the mail man and this one I am going to put on their letter. I left Farmang yesterday noon just when they were marching into dinner I went
downtown because I had to meet them there at noon or after. I hope you had good luck yesterday. Well, Charles, I have to stop now. Haven't got nothing else to say. But you must remember I am going to be your friend forever even if you don't care for me. I read my heart belongs to Wilfred and you only.

I am your friend,

P.S. Remember you have a friend that MacWright will forget you she will think of you as long as she can't see her love of you earthy.

The law provides:
"That hereafter no appropriation, except appropriations made pursuant to treaties, shall be used to educate children of less than one-fourth Indian blood whose parents are citizens of the United States and of the State wherein they live and where there are adequate free school facilities provided." (Act of May 25, 1918 (40 Stat. L., 564).)

EXTRACT FROM SCHOOL RULES.

If the facilities of a school are not needed as provided in the above paragraph, children showing any Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, and who if debarred from Government schools could not obtain an education, may be enrolled in the day and boarding schools of their reservation; these children, however, may not be transferred to nonreservation schools without special permission from the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

Before enrolling a pupil superintendents must satisfy themselves whether he has left any other school without permission and whether he has been expelled for cause from any other school. A pupil who has been expelled for cause from any Indian school may be reenrolled in any Indian school only with permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Attention is invited to Circulars Nos. 1039 and 1044, which must be observed.

Indian children whose parents are amply able to pay for their education and who have public school facilities at or near their homes shall not hereafter be enrolled in Government Indian Schools supported by gratuity appropriations, except on payment of actual per capita cost and transportation. (Sec. 6, Declaration of Policy, dated April 17, 1917.)

My dear Mr. Hell:

Replying to yours of November 18th:

Mr. J. W. Evans of Koyukuk Station, Alaska, wrote and asked that the boys be allowed to continue in school until spring. This was in reply to my letter requesting tuition from him.

I have authority from the Commissioner to retain the Alaskan pupils that I have already enrolled until the end of the school year. I will talk Chemawa to them and will do my best to influence them to enter it at the close of their term here next spring.

They are now 8th grade boys, 13 to 17 years of age and are very desirable pupils.

Very truly yours,

Superintendent.

My dear Mr. Evans:

I beg to acknowledge receipt of your letter regarding your three boys.

The Commissioner has approved my retaining them until the end of the present school year, when they will graduate from this school. That does not mean, however, that their education will be complete but I strongly urge you to have them enter Chautauqua in order to complete the full Vocational course which will carry them through the 9th and 10th grades.

Wilfred and Charles have completed two years in Engineering and are now enrolled in the third year. It will be very much to their advantage to review the third year next year and to take the 4th year as they are especially interested in that kind of work.

Richard is at present in grade six and has had a taste of carpentry, blacksmithing, painting, and gardening. He is not particularly interested in anything yet except his music and having a good time.

Very sincerely yours,

[signed] Superintendent.
