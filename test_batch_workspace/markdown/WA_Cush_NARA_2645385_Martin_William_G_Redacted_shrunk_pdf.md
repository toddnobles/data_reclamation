Washington

Dear Madam:

I have your letter of April 16 making further inquiries about your son's trouble. I sympathize with you in the burden of sorrow you feel.

It appears that a number of thefts committed by some of the boys at this school were reported to the Tacoma police and that several boys were taken by the police in the early part of the year. These boys admitted the thefts and were excused on the promise to do no more stealing. But about a month ago, the owner of a store near the school, complained to the police that some of the Indian boys from this school had broken into the store and had stolen a quantity of goods.

The police located the boys who admitted to the theft, and took them before the juvenile court in Tacoma. Three of the boys, one of whom was your boy, were given sentences from one to fifteen years each in the State Reform School. Your son and another boy, being over 16 years of age, were sent to Monroe, Washington.

I suggest that you write your boy an encouraging letter and ask him to obey all the rules of the Reformatory and by so doing he may be able to get released in the shortest possible time. Otherwise, if he is refractory, he may be required to serve out the full sentence.

I regret very much that these boys were guilty of the violation of the law, as this institution has constantly urged upon the pupils the necessity of obeying not only the rules of the school, but the laws of the State.

Very respectfully,

Special Agent in Charge.

The law provides:
"That no part of this appropriation, or any other appropriation provided for herein except appropriations made pursuant to treaties, shall be used to educate children of less than one-fourth Indian blood whose parents are citizens of the United States and of the State wherein they live and where there are adequate free-school facilities provided and the facilities of the Indian schools are needed for pupils of more than one-fourth Indian blood." (Act of August 1, 1914.)

EXTRACTS FROM SCHOOL RULES.

If the facilities of a school are not needed as provided in the above paragraph, children showing any Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, and who if debarred from Government schools could not obtain an education, may be enrolled in the day and boarding schools of their reservation; these children, however, may not be transferred to nonreservation schools without special permission from the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

Before enrolling a pupil superintendents must satisfy themselves whether he has left any other school without permission and whether he has been expelled for cause from any other school. A pupil who has been expelled for cause from any Indian school may be reenrolled in any Indian school only with permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

"The Secretary of the Interior may in his discretion withhold rations, clothing, and other annuities from Indian parents or guardians who refuse to or neglect to send or keep their children of proper school age in some school during a reasonable portion of each year." (27 Stat. L., 635. See 2 Dec. Comp., 308.)
