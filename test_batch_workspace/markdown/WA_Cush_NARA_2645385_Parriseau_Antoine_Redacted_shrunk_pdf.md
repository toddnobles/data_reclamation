DEPARTMENT OF THE INTERIOR
UNITED STATES INDIAN SERVICE

Flathead Agency - Dixon, Mont.

April 17, 1920.

C. V. Peel, Special Agent in Charge,
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Mr. Peel:

Your letter of April 12, 1920, concerning the desire of Gus White that the Parizeau boys be sent home to assist him in work, is received.

It is not believed essential nor desirable that the Parizeau boys should be returned to assist Gus White, who is their stepfather. Gus White, it is well known in the community in which he lives, does very little work, and he certainly does not need the assistance of these young boys to help him do the little labor which he now performs.

Your letter, as above referred to, is the first intimation this office has had that Patrick Parizeau has been placed in a reform school at Monroe. For our files, will you kindly advise me the circumstances under which this boy has been placed in the reform school, and the length of term to which he has been sentenced?

Very truly yours,

Theodore Sharp
Superintendent.

TS/R

Cushman Indian School
Tacoma, Wash., April 22, 1920

Theodore Sharp, Esq.
Flathead Indian Agency
Dixon, Montana

Dear Mr. Sharp:

I have your inquiry of April 17, 1920, concerning Patrick Parizeau and in reply thereto will state that he appears to have a decided weakness for stealing; that he has been punished at different times for his thefts at the school. In the early part of this year, he was apprehended by the Tacoma police for stealing some property from a cabin, some distance from the school. He admitted the theft, but was not prosecuted. The juvenile officer took no action, but advised Pat that if he was found guilty of any more thefts he would eventually land in the reform school.

But Pat could not resist stealing. He, with three other boys, broke into a store near the school and appropriated all the goods they could carry away. The police took them in charge and haled them into the juvenile Court, where Pat and another boy were sentenced to the Reform School at Monroe, Washington, for a term of one to fifteen years. One of the other two boys was sentenced to the Reform School at Chelan, but the sentence was suspended upon the application of one of our day school teachers who voluntarily assumed the responsibility for this boy's future conduct.

The fourth boy escaped punishment by the Court.

Very respectfully,

Special Agent in Charge.

Ronan Mont
Sept. 1, 1919

Dear Sir,

Just a line or two.

Mr. Hammond, I would like to know if my boy Antoine Parisian is still there. I don't get any answers from him. I write to him and he doesn't answer.

How is he and Louise? I hope they are all well and happy.

My boy Pat doesn't seem to like Cushman. I don't believe I'll send him to school this fall. He's going to saw wood for
me this winter.

Hope the school is getting along alright.

must close. hoping to hear from you.

Yours Respectfully,
Mrs Angelic White
Ronan Mont

Box #3710
Cushman Indian School  
Tacoma, Wash., Sept. 11, 1919

Mrs. Angelic White  
Box 370  
Ronan, Montana

Dear Mrs. White:

I received your letter and Pat's regarding his return to school.

I have sufficient of Pat's money on deposit here to send an employee for him in case he doesn't return promptly and I shall use Pat's money for this purpose unless he does return. Louis and Antoine are at Cushman and are well.

It is very dangerous for a boy of Pat's age to be allowed to do as he pleases and it would be for his advantage to learn that there is a higher law than his own wishes. Please advise Pat that unless he returns within a week, I shall use his money to return him to school.

Very sincerely yours,

Superintendent.

July 25, 1917

Dear son Antonio,

Just a few lines to let you know I am home. Give the boys my best regards how are they getting along and also Mrs. and Mr. Barton.

I stopped in town creek and fought fire for a while.

Its Mr. Brown we half to see now for you to come home, don't spend any of your money in the office yet you got a chance to some how yet. Pat said did you get them pictures developed yet send them as soon as you can.

We need your boys home. Keep on asking Mr. Hammond if you can tell him to write to Mrs. Brown we're going to the garden.

Yours close with love and regard.

I remains your truly mother

Angelic P. White
