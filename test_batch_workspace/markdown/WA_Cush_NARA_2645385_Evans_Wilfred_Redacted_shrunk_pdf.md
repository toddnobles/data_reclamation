INDORSEMENT.

A child showing one-sixteenth or less Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, Indian fashion, who, if debarred from the Government schools, could not obtain an education, may be permitted in the reservation day and boarding schools, but it is preferable that it be not transferred to a nonreservation school, without special permission from the Office. Children showing one-eighth or less Indian blood, whose parents do not live on an Indian reservation, whose home is among white people where there are churches and schools, who are presumed to have adopted the white man's manners and customs, and are to all intents and purposes white people, are debarred from enrollment in the Government nonreservation and reservation schools. Superintendents, in all cases where doubt exists as to the degree of Indian blood of a child proposed for transfer, should fully satisfy themselves of the facts by affidavits from reliable persons, which affidavits must be kept on file at the school.

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both Superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and Superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

A pupil dismissed from school for cause must not be enrolled in any other school without the permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Full facts must be submitted with each request.

Supt. Cushman School
Tacoma Wash.

Dear Sir; - Your letter of May 23 just received and glad to hear from you. We knew nothing of Wilfred loosing his rank and am very sorry to hear of his seeming lack of interest.

In one letter he said he thought he would like to learn to run the store and since it is impossible to get away for even a day unless we hire someone to look after things and often it is difficult to find anyone we can trust, his request seemed to solve the difficulty so we wired.

I really think they are better off in school in the states and if it had not presented a solution to the work problem here would not have wired. Transportation had to be taken into consideration and we have very poor boat accommodations this year that it best to have them come in early.

I can readily see how a different interpretation could have been placed upon the telegram but such was not done intentionally.

I am anxious to have our boys a credit to the school and trust that all difficulties and misunderstandings have been adjusted.

Respectfully,

J.W. Evans

"Do you boys want to come home. If so let us know and will make arrangements with West Coast Grocery Company to buy your tickets. You can attend school here.

(Signed) J. W. Evans

The telegram was sent from Koyukuk, Alaska, May 21, 1919.

Your sons have spent two full school years at Cushman and I am glad to report they have taken advantage of opportunities offered them in their academic and industrial work, and I feel sure you will agree that they have been benefited by the two years spent here.

I suppose Wilfred has become disgruntled at having lost his commission of Captain of Company C, due to his neglect of duties as a captain. He was dis-rated but was advised that his reinstatement as captain was awaiting him and I would be glad to reinstate him if he showed a greater interest in company formations, etc.

The experience as an officer is most valuable as it increases a boy's sense of responsibility and develops and strengthens his character.

Your children were enrolled at Cushman for a period of three years. Next year, Wilfred and Charles will complete the eighth grade which is as far as this school carries the children, altho I am in hopes authority will be granted to establish a nineth grade, or third year vocational, beginning next September. Inasmuch as you possess school facilities for your boys, there will be no objection to the cancellation of your contract and enrollment of your boys at Cushman before the expiration of the three year enrollment period, providing that you forfeit to the Government the cost of the tuition, board, and clothing for the three of them during the past two years, at the rate of $200.00 per annum, the actual cost to the Government for each pupil enrolled in an Indian boarding school.

Very respectfully,
Mr. Hammond:

Wilfred Evans positively will not report to Miss Carlonougher for training in the declamation contest. She has exhausted all means to get him to do as he should in learning it etc. He deserves no truks or anything nice. You have simply been mean about it, and as you know, he is capable.

My Dear Mr. Commissioner:

I beg to acknowledge receipt of your form 5-385 with letter from Wilfred Evans attached.

Wilfred is a vocational pupil, grade VII, Cushman Trades School. During vacation, he and his brother, Charles, were the only vocational engineers at school, consequently when the laundry was run, they were detailed to work in the boiler room—but under the supervision of Mr. LeRoy, the school fireman.

Wilfred and Charles were detailed as pupils receiving board, lodging and clothing during the vacation period and whenever they were placed on all day detail as happened twice to my knowledge, a week at a time, they were given in return leave of absence to work in the country, on a wage of $3.50 a day to repay them for their extra time on duty at school.

Wilfred has taken advantage of the opportunities offered him, and his knowledge of steam engineering does credit to his earnestness of purpose. He is not however competent to run the plant without supervision, least of all to fill the place of the Chief Engineer.

On Saturday, September 7th, Wilfred shut down the plant without authority and before the Dining room detail had finished with the dishes. He had dressed himself for a visit to the city without previous authority and was naturally disgusted when I required him to hold steam until 1:45 to permit the girls to finish their work.

He has several times spoken in a way to show that he thought the government owed him for vacation service in addition to his board, lodging and clothing. I had explained that he was too young to be enrolled among the employees—that when he was older and more proficient it might be possible.

Wilfred is all right but he exemplifies the old adage that "A little knowledge is a dangerous thing."

Very respectfully,

Superintendent
Cushman School,
Tacoma, Wash.
Sept. 8, 1918.

Hon. Cato Sells,
Washington, D. C.

Dear Sir:

I am only sixteen years old and have been holding the chief engineer's position almost all summer.

The superintendent put the responsibility of the whole boiler room on me and my smaller brother, fire up all day. We even worked eleven hours for several days and still he wanted us to work longer.

I asked him why I did not get paid for my work he said the government would not allow it.

If I work eight hours a day and hold the chief engineer's position, I do not see why I should not get paid for it.

That is all at present and I close my letter with hopes for a favorable reply.

Wilfred Evans
