Colville Agency, Nespelem, Wash.,
July 12, 1919.

Supt. E. H. Hammond,
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Mr. Hammond:

Mrs. William Bell, aunt of Mary Herron, requests that Mary be permitted to return to the Reservation for the vacation period. She stated that it was rumored that Mary had returned to Addy or vicinity, which is a point twenty miles east of the eastern boundary line of the Colville Reservation. If Mary is still at your school, do you think it advisable for her to return for the vacation? I presume you know something of Mary's past history. It is a question in my mind whether it is for her best interests to return, but I will leave the matter largely to your judgement. Her aunt is interested in knowing what privileges Mary has in getting out or visiting the city. I assured her that Mary, or any other student, would be permitted to go out at reasonable times with one of the matrons or employee of your school. Please ascertain if Mary is desirous of returning and let me know what you think regarding the matter.

Yours very truly,

Fred C. Morgan
Superintendent

DEPARTMENT OF THE INTERIOR

Colville Agency, Nespelem, Wash.,
June 16, 1919.

Supt. E. H. Hammond,
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Mr. Hammond:

This refers to your letter of recent date regarding the application of the parents of Juanita Joe for her return home for the vacation period. I have just had a talk with Joe and his wife regarding your suggestion that she remain at the school during the vacation period, and I regret to say that her parents will not consent to this.

Joe is a very good Indian and has always taken an interest in sending his children to school and I am sure that Joe will want her returned when school begins in the Fall.

If you will refer to the files of this case, you will find that her father has been trying to get her into your school for the past two years. This shows his attitude towards the school matters and especially your school.

I respectfully recommend that the parent's request be granted, and Juanita permitted to return at the earliest practicable date.

Very truly yours,

Fred C. Morgan
Superintendent.

Cushman Indian School
Tacoma, Wash., July 17, 1919

Mr. Fred C. Morgan, Supt.
Colville Indian Agency
Hespelem, Wash.

Dear Mr. Morgan:

Replying to your letter of July 12 regarding the whereabouts of Mary Herron, Mary is still at Cushman and I think it advisable for her to remain here as the chances are that if she returns home at this late date, she will not be ready to return in the Fall.

I know nothing of Mary's past history, but if she has a history, it is all the more reason why she should remain at school here.

Please advise her aunt that Mary is entitled to visit the city of Tacoma twice a month, in company with the Matron or one of the teachers. She has not gone recently, however, as she has no spending money. Mary really wishes to return home but she is not unhappy here and has just enough work to do, as hospital nurse, to keep her out of mischief.

I am afraid I neglected to answer your letter of June 16 regarding Jaunita Joe's return to Colville for the vacation period. Jaunita is not enrolled at this school. It may be that she is at Chemawa.

Sincerely yours,

Superintendent.

May 12, 1913

Dear Mrs. La Fleur:

I have Alice's suitcase in the office containing all her effects except the dress she was wearing. As I have not searched her effects, I do not know whether there is any money or not in the suitcase. I will send the key parcel post or express within a few days, or as soon as I can get Mary Herron to check the effects.

Michelinius is so far away that I do not think it would be advisable for Mary to go home for the very short time. Moreover, I have found that in so many cases where the
Children return home at Xmas, they fall sick either at home, or on their way to school. The long vacation will soon arrive—it is only six months away and it would be much better for many to wait until then. She will be spared another attack of homesickness and will enjoy her vacation all the more.

Sincerely yours,
Cushman Indian School,
Tucson, Ariz., Oct. 8, 1918.

The Honorable,
The Commissioner of Indian Affairs,
Washington, D.C.

My Dear Mr. Commissioner:

I have the honor to report three cases of scarlet fever at the Cushman School. One taken September 7th, the second September 25th, and the third September 29th. These children and four contacts have been isolated and carefully observed. The three cases of scarlet fever are out of danger, and the four cases under observation show no signs of contracting the disease.

Dr. Kincaid and I have taken every precaution to prevent the spread of scarlet fever. These cases are sporadic and were undoubtedly introduced by new pupils.

The hospital and the girls' buildings have been thoroughly fumigated. From the history of the cases, the Doctor does not fear an epidemic, and every precaution will be observed to prevent one.

Very respectfully,

Superintendent

SPECIAL NOTE.
This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All the blanks must be properly filled in every case.
If the information called for on any part of the blank is not known, that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. Whether the parents are living or dead, that fact should be given.
The person who signs the certificate stating the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

Applicable laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

Compliance must be given to all orders and instructions promulgated in connection with the new course of study issued December 1, 1915.

The information as to public school facilities called for in the certificate of the Superintendent (third page hereof) must be given, and all superintendents are especially directed to exhaust every means to secure enrollment of Indian children in the State public schools, pursuant to instructions heretofore issued in this regard.
