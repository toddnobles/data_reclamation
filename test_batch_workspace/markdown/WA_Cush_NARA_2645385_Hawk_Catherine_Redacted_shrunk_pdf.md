SPECIAL NOTE.
This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All the blanks must be properly filled out in every case.
If a child is not called for on any part of the blank is not known, that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. Whether the child is living or dead, their names must be given.
The person who signs the blank as consenting to the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

The laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

This form is to be used only in transfers from reservations or Indian schools to nonreservation schools.

Jan, 2nd, 1920

Mr. E.H. Hammond,
Supt Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Washington.

Dear Sir,

I have your letter of the 24th inst., as to the vacation leave of pupils.

After further investigation on this Catherine Hawk case I am satisfied that there was small basis for the complaint made Mrs Cole, and it was a personal difficulty between herself and Husband and the girl secondary to an end.

I have placed the girl in the school here and the parents have been made to understand that will be no trifling in the matter as to her school attendance.

I am enclosing my personal check for money paid to me by them to send to your office, $60.00 dollars.

Hattie Major and Lizzie Moses should have arrived at the school in good time as I started them from here on Tuesday to arrive at the school on Wednesday. I trust that they arrived safely.

Simon Charley is an indian who is not very faithful to his promises. I have no objection to his returning the children here if he does so in a proper manner. Charlie's main object however seems to get the children first and arrange other matters as he sees fit.

Have they arrived yet?

Taholah Washington
Dec. 18th, 1919.

Mr. E H Hammond
Supt., Cushman Indian School
Tacoma Washington.

Dear Sir,

I am sending a request under other cover that you can act on as seems best to you. This seems a reversal of the position taken by the former Supt, in this matter. Case of Catherine Hawks, pupil at the school at present.

From a careful and thorough investigation of this case which seems to have assumed some importance in the tribe I am in doubt as to the correctness of some of the charges made in connection with the matter. There is a great deal of reputable evidence that there is slight and unconfirmed basis as to the accusations made. Of course you are familiar through the letters of the former Supt. as to this case. If you think it would be a detriment to her to come home for the Holidays I would prefer that you retain her. Otherwise I would like very much to see and talk with her personally. I would like to obtain through your office uninfluenced by the Mother just what she wishes to do as remaining here or returning to the school. I have expressed no opinion to them that there is any chance of her not returning to Cushman and if you so desire return she will.
At any rate I am willing to approve the action you may take in the matter as your judgement as to conduct etc of the girl will be much better than mine.

Very Respectfully,

Eugene W. Klee
Supt., & Phy.

TAHOLAH INDIAN AGENCY,  
Taholah, Washington, Nov. 11, 1919.

FOR FILE

Supt. E. H. Hammond,  
Cushman Trades School, Tacoma, Wn.

My dear Mr. Hammond:

Replying to your letters relative to the release of Catherine Hawk, or Cole, you are advised that I am opposed to the release of the girl. Julia Cole brought her to my house one morning and the place for her is in a boarding school.

Yesterday Julia came to my office with the usual whimper that she can not get along without Catherine. I tried to make it plain that I would not consent to the girl's return. Her plea that she will put Catherine in the public school at Moclips is only a ruse to get her home. I told her that if she brought the girl home I would immediately take the matter up with the state authorities and endeavor to have her taken from her authority. I believe this course justified by the conditions under which the girl is being brought up, and I will request that you learn what officer has jurisdiction over such juvenile cases and let me know. Then if Julia does not settle down and discontinue bothering you and me, and disturbing the girl in her school work, I will see if something can not be done by the state courts. Enough of this is plenty.

You probably have seen enough of the Indians belonging over this way to form a judgment of the happy, happy time I have spent with them. I hope to leave in about two weeks, however, and I transfer whatever of good will I have remaining to my successor without doubt of my ability to control my emotions when I depart, unless my joy is too much in the ascendancy, when I may give a few hoots!

Very respectfully,  
Supt.
