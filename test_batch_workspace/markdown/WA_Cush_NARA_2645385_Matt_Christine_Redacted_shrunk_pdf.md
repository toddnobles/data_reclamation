Capt. E.H. Hammond,

Dear Sir,

Just a few lines concerning my little daughter, Crissie. I have heard that she was troubled with St. Vitus dance, now about a month ago. I am really surprised that you have never notified us, as you are Superintendent of the school, and I think it is your duty to do so. Now Mr. Hammond she has been troubled before, and we were strictly advised by a physician to keep her where it was quite, and could be all by herself without children to excite her, so you see that school is no place for her.

Please kindly let me hear by
return mail if you can send her home, also if I can get Way for vacation.

Hoping to hear from you at your earliest convenience.

I remain

Yours truly,

Margaret Matt
Cushman Indian School  
Tacoma, Wash., June 13, 1919

Mrs. Margaret Matt  
Evaro, Montana

Dear Mrs. Matt:

I am in receipt of your letter of June 8, 1919, regarding the condition of your daughter Christine.

Mrs. Culbertson, the School Nurse, informs me that Christine is very nervous, but that she is getting along as well as could be expected. Christine has not been in school for some time. She has been staying at the Hospital where it is quiet and the other children do not excite her.

Christine and May may come home for the vacation if you will sign the enclosed Vacation Request and return it to this office by return mail.

Very sincerely yours,

Superintendent.

May 29, 1919.

E. H. Hammond, Superintendent,
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Washington.

Dear Sir;-

Frank Matt, a brother of Christine Matt, who is enrolled in your school, advises me that information has been received by his father's family that his sister, Christine, is afflicted with what is commonly known as St. Vitus' Dance, and is said to be in a serious condition.

Will you please advise me at once just what the condition of this girl is, and I would thank you to send me a report from your school physician, so that the friends of the child may be fully informed as to the state of her health.

Very truly yours,

[signature]
Superintendent.

Dixon, Montana:

My dear Mr. Sharp:

I wrote the parents of Christine Matt regarding her condition and I was under the impression that I had also advised you. Christine is improving very much, especially since school was disbanded, June 15th. She has been suffering from an extreme attack of nervousness which culminated in St. Vitus' Dance, brought on by a shock which she experienced when a flash light picture of her department was taken. She is getting fat and the Doctor anticipates no serious consequences and advises me that the girl will recover in due course of time.

Of course you know her home environment better than I and are in a position to determine whether she will recover more quickly at home than at school.

May and her brothers are very well.

Very respectfully,

Superintendent.

SPECIAL NOTE.
This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. If he does not, he should be promptly filled out by any official.

If the information called for on any part of the blank is not known, that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. Whether the parents are living or dead, their names must be given.

The person who signs the blank as consenting to the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

Applicable laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

Compliance must be given to all orders and instructions promulgated in connection with the new course of study issued December 1, 1915.

The information as to public school facilities called for in the certificate of the Superintendent (third page hereof) must be given, and all superintendents are especially directed to exhaust every means to secure enrollment of Indian children in the State public schools, pursuant to instructions heretofore issued in this regard.
