Supt. E. H. Hammond,
Cushman Trades School,
Tacoma, Washington.

Dear Sir:

Your letter of January 28 is received.

I am very much grieved to learn about Byron. I cannot understand why he should have done such a thing, and I do not see how he came to learn to do such a thing. A great many times the fault in matters of this kind lies not so much with the child as with the system of discipline and the disciplinarian. I am not trying to insinuate that this is the case now, but I am sure you realize a disciplinarian has much to do with the moral uplift of the child. A disciplinarian, especially in a school like Cushman, must be a man of sterling quality, broad experience in the management of children; a man thoroughly versed in child welfare work; he must be a psychologist as well. Unfortunately, the Indian School service is much hampered in its work by the lack of just such men. We frequently blame the child for going wrong, when, if we will but look deeper into the situation, we will find the fault lies either with the system of discipline practiced or with the disciplinarian himself. From your statement, Byron has gone wrong. There is no doubt about this, and now is the time to correct him. I can do it, I am sure. At the same time, I do not believe you should censure the boy too severely, but it might be well, not only for his sake, but also for the sake of those who remain at Cushman, to inquire into the system of discipline and the competence of your
disciplinarian to cope with conditions such as this which are frequently found in Indian schools.

I feel that I am to a degree, at least, competent to write in this strain for the reason I have had much experience in Indian school work - not only with the child, but with the employee as well. I know the deficiencies of a great many of the employees in the Indian Service. It is a wonder to me that more of the children in Indian schools do not go wrong. One thing I have always found to be true - you must keep temptation away from the child. It makes no difference whether the child is Indian, white, or any other race. A child will go wrong unless those whose duty it is to see that every precaution is taken to safeguard him does this very thing. Disciplinarians in the Indian Service are extremely few and very far apart. I once knew a disciplinarian in the Indian Service whose work was the best I had ever seen (and I have known many disciplinarians in the Service). He had absolute control over the boys, and the girls as well. He had the honor system working over time. He took one of the most incorrigible boys in school, and he happened to be the largest, and larger and stronger than the disciplinarian - and he taught this boy noble things, brought him to see his life in a different light, and that boy became the most trusted boy in school, and he did not dishonor or abuse the trust. It was not imprisonment or harshness that worked this change - it was common sense, the knowledge of the make-up of the individual. Many of the children wept when he left, yet he was forced out of the Service because of rottenness in other directions. And while I upheld him in his work, and knew he was a good man, it was my painful duty to bring pressure to bear to
oust this disciplinarian for fear he would eventually cause a looseness in the school. And so I say, if your discipline is not right; if your disciplinarian is not filling his position, you may always look for such trouble, and probably worse. Chemawa school was a notable institution for laxity of discipline, and maybe Byron got his start there. Mind, Mr. Hammond, I am not saying that the above conditions exist at your school, and I do not know that they do not. As one who has children at your school it is my privilege to request to be advised along these lines. We can not avoid trouble by running from it or by shifting the responsibility. The thing to do is to get at the root of the evil, and when Byron is with me this is just what I intend to do. And when I have found out the circumstances leading up to this warping of his mind - when it should have been cultured, I imagine I will start something. If the Indian Rights Association is to be of any use to the Indian, now is the time for it to act, and this is just the line of work it should take up.

I should certainly like to have my boy with me, but just at the present time I am not financially able to pay his transportation here. I am taking up with the Indian Office the matter of transferring my children to the Albuquerque school, and I will advise you in regard to the matter. For the present please retain him under such discipline as you deem best. I do not feel ill toward you, Mr. Hammond, but I know you will understand my feelings in this matter.

Yours very truly,

A.M. Caisse

Dear Sir:

Your son, Byron, stole a twelve dollar check and erased the names of the payer and payee, substituting his own name and yours in their stead. He was, of course, detected on trying to cash it and was, of course, punished with confinement in the school jail for a week. He was released yesterday on his promise to keep straight and kept his word by running off sometime during the night.

I dislike very much to have Byron sent to a reform school, but it seems impossible to break him of the habit of stealing and kindred vices.

The only hope for Byron is for you to take him in charge yourself. You may be able to arouse a manly spirit and habits of common honesty. Otherwise, I am afraid Byron will become a confirmed crook. We done everything we could to show him his danger and have appealed to the best in him time and time again without effect.

If you will refund the school the expense of his recapture, we will get him and will send him to you at your expense.

Very respectfully,
