Colville Agency, Nespelem, Wash.,
July 1, 1919.

Superintendent E. H. Hammond,
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Mr. Hammond:

You failed to enclose copy of the correspondence you had with reference to the trio, who were guilty of check raising. Your letter, however, contains sufficient information. Since the girls agree to remain in school until excused by the banker, I am of the opinion that they should remain with you during the vacation, otherwise they will be getting off too easy. I respectfully suggest that you write the parents of Mary James and Collette Martin, advising them that the girls are contented, well and getting along very nicely. This will have a tendency to put them at ease. Then perhaps they will cease bombarding you with requests for the girls to return for the vacation. I will leave the matter wholly to your judgement.

Yours very truly,

Fred C. Morgan
Superintendent

The only part that I can learn that Colett Martin took in the matter was that she advised Mary to raise the check, stating that it could be done and they would not be caught. I have not been able to see Colett, but as soon as I have had an interview with her I will write you again.

Mary James has agreed to return to Cushman and explain the entire transaction, of course I advised her to do this, telling her it was my opinion that if she were truthful in the matter that you would assist her in having the matter properly adjusted with bank officials.

Very respectfully,

Fred C. Morgan
Superintendent.

CUSHMAN INDIAN SCHOOL, TACOMA, WASH.

January 15, 1919.

I, Viola Runnels, do hereby voluntarily make the following statements which are true to the best of my knowledge.

We, (Nancy & Mary James, Mary Iswald, Colette Martin and myself) were up in the dormitory. We were going to run away Sunday night but didn't get away until Monday morning, October 2d. Mary James said that she never had enough money and didn't know what she could do with a check for only $5. Then she said; "Let us change it into $50." I said: "Would we get caught?" and she said: "No, because I will change my name different and we won't get caught." She then wanted to know what we would do and I told her we would do anything that you will and so did Mary Iswald. Then she said, "How will we rub the $5. and make it $50. I can put the naught right by the five and make it fifty." I told her it was all right and she asked Mary Iswald, and she told her the same. Then she took a knife and scraped the "e" in five and changed it to fifty. Then she changed the name on the check to Margaret Jameson and signed it on the back that way. I went downstairs then but the check was fixed before I went away.

The morning we ran away, we went out the end of the grounds and Mary James led the way around the streets until we came to the Puyallup road then we took a bus and got to Puyallup. We didn't know where we wanted to go but finally we took another bus to Sumner. When we got to Sumner we asked the way to the depot and Mary James asked me to take the check in and get it cashed. When I asked the agent to cash the check he asked me how much it was for and I told him fifty dollars. He said he couldn't cash such a large check. Mary James had some silver and greenbacks besides the check and when I couldn't get it cashed she said we would go to get something to eat and then buy some hats. She and I wanted to buy a hat. When we got through eating, we went to buy the hats and after we had them picked out, she didn't have enough money to pay for my hat so I took the check to buy the hat with. The lady asked us whose check it was and I said it was Mary James. Then Mary James paid the lady for my hat and took the money herself. She gave me $10 and she gave Mary Iswald $10, but when we got outside again, she took my $10 and said I might lose it. Mary Iswald did not have anything to do with the raising of the check but she was present when the check was cashed and shared in the sum of $10. When Mary James divided the money, she said "Now if we get caught, you will each have to pay $10."

Viola Runnels
Cushman Indian School,

SPECIAL NOTE.

This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All the blanks must be properly filled in every case.

If the information called for on any part of the blank is not known, that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. Whether the parents are living or dead, their names must be given.

The person who signs the blank as consenting to the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

The laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

This form is to be used only in transfers from reservations or Indian schools to nonreservation schools.
