The Fee Patents arrived that you kindly forwarded yesterday after letter mailed to you. Delayed somewhere on the way. The mail delivery to this station seems to rest quite frequently, as far as Moclips station is concerned, upon the discretion of the Postmaster.

DEPARTMENT OF THE INTERIOR
UNITED STATES INDIAN SERVICE
TAHOLAH INDIAN AGENCY,
Taholah, Washington, December 15, 1918.

Supt. E. S. Hammond,
Cushman Trades School, Tacoma.

Dear Sir:

It has come to my notice that Lizzie Moses and Hattie Major are planning to come home for the holiday vacation.

For reasons already given you, it is altogether better for these girls to be kept away from here as long as possible. Besides those reasons, I consider it inadvisable for them to come on account of the hazard of introducing influenza here.

I have learned that Hattie has planned - with Lizzie's acquiescence in the plan- for her dead sister's husband, Mitchell Sam, with whom she corresponds, and a young married man, Charles Sampson, of Queets River, to come to Tacoma and there meet the girls. You can surmise the rest. I would like to suggest that if these fellows show up there you lay a trap for them and have them turned over to the police. No punishment you can give them will be half sufficient.

Very truly yours,

Otis B. Benson, Supt.
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Wash., Nov. 13, 1918.

Dr. Otis O. Benson, Supt.
Taholah, Washington.

Dear Doctor:

Replying to your of November 11th, I have had all correspondence to Lizzie Moses and Hattie Major carefully censured since your advice of last August and have intercepted and destroyed several "bad" letters. If any more arrive, I will forward them to the postal authorities for their action.

These girls are obedient and modest in deportment and have given us no trouble so far. Next spring I shall endeavor for their sakes to retain them in school. If their parents stay away, I feel sure I can hold the girls.

Addie Sam has had a serious attack of Spanish influenza and has been slow to recuperate. Last week she had several slight hemorrhages which were reported to our Doctor who advised me to allow Addie Sam to return home on leave of absence without delay. Addie left for home Monday, Nov. 11, 1918.

Very respectfully,

Superintendent

July 29th, 1918.

Otis O. Benson.
Superintendent & Physician.
Taholah, Washington.

Dear Mr. Benson:

Hattie Major was allowed to return home for the vacation period on condition that she return promptly September 5th, to Cushman at her own expense.

Hattie was a very good girl while at school and I very much regret hearing that she is suspected of such a trick.

As the date for re-opening school is rapidly drawing near, I will appreciate any effort you may make to interest your children in Cushman, which is their home school. Owing to the excessive high price of printing this year, I have not sent out a prospectus of the school but assure you that the full, prescribed course from grades 1 thru 8 are given in the letter and the spirit. There are a number of boys in your jurisdiction who have been, or should be enrolled here. If you will kindly send me their names and addresses I shall be glad to write them personally.

Very sincerely yours,

Superintendent.

SPECIAL NOTE.
This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All blank spaces must be properly filled in entirely in ink. If the information calls for an entry of "black" or "white," that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. Whether the parents are living or dead, their names must be given.
The person who signs the blank as consenting to the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

The laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

This form is to be used only in transfers from reservations or Indian schools to nonreservation schools.
