SPECIAL NOTE.
This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All the blanks must be properly filled in every case.
If the information called for on any part of the blank is not known, that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. Whether the parents are living or dead should be clearly given.
The person who signs the blank pertaining to the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

The laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

This form is to be used only in transfers from reservations or Indian schools to nonreservation schools.

Dear Mr. Peel:

Peter Moses and Patsy Moses are returning to Cushman, and left this afternoon for the railway. Peter came home to spend the holidays and Patsy with several other boys deserted from the Cushman school some two years ago, before finishing their enrolment. Patsy has been acting as interpreter at this agency during the past two winters, and we have found him very trustworthy for a boy of his years.

Peter and Patsy will probably take up with you the matter of Eddie Moses, a cousin, being permitted to return to Cushman. Eddie deserted Cushman twice, also wrote Superintendent Hammond a very obscene letter.

At first I was disposed to comply with the boys' request that I recommend that Eddie be permitted to return to your school, but after considering the proposition from every angle, and also the fact that he very much desired to return with Peter and Patsy, I know of no more effective punishment to administer to him than to deny his request to return. I believe as a matter of discipline he should not be permitted to return; however, the matter is left with you. If you consider it advisable for him to return, let me suggest that you tell the boys, in the event they present the matter, that owing to the lateness of the school year as well as to Eddie's past record he had better wait and try to enter some non-reservation school at the beginning of the next school year.

Mr. Peel.

It is my honest impression that the other two boys, Peter and Patay, will be better off and more contented without Eddie.

I do not care to have the boys know that I have written this and respectfully request that you consider this letter as confidential.

Very truly yours,

Fred C. O'Donnell
Superintendent.

Dear Mr. Morgan:

Peter and Nancy Moses are well and, to all appearances enjoying themselves.

Peter is one of our Captains, and one of our star players on the school football team. Dear Sir:

I enclose herewith vacation requests for Peter Moses of Nespelem, and Mike Quill of Covada. Both are for sick leave, and the requests are signed by the boys.

Please have the parents sign the requests and return them to this office at your earliest convenience.

The Doctor has recommended that Peter Moses remain at home on account of his glands until February, when the rainy and wet season here is practically over. Moses has deposited the necessary return fare.

Mike Quill, who was ill with pneumonia last spring and returned home as soon as he was able to travel, came back this year and was taken sick a few days after his return. My dear Mr. Morgan:

Your letter of March 12 is hereby acknowledged.

For Peter's sake I am sorry his father has asked for leave at this time, as Peter will find it very difficult to make his grade after a three-weeks absence.

I am enclosing a request for vacation leave for Peter's father to sign and to return with a deposit as required therein, and if the father will agree faithfully to return Peter at the expiration of the vacation leave, he may go for the three weeks.

I realize the importance of the agricultural work among the Indians, especially during these abnormal times, and wish to do all I can to cooperate with you and your Indians. Leave of absence, as I have said, is a very serious matter with the pupils at this time of the year, because the leave will interfere seriously with the review work necessary to whip in the loose ends of instruction, and to prepare the child for a successful completion of the year's work.

Peter's father has undoubtedly just reasons for making the request, and it would not matter much if he were the only one involved, but I do not wish to set a precedent in the case of Peter and his father.

Very respectfully,

Superintendent.
