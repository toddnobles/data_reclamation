Dear Friend:

This is to advise you that your grandson Fredie Nostrum is sick with pulmonary tuberculosis. He now has a cold, and should he contract influenza and pneumonia, he will have but little chance to recover.

Yours very sincerely,

TO ALL MEMBERS OF THE CUSHMAN STUDENT ASSOCIATION

Dear Friends:

Nearly two years have passed since our last Reunion and Conference and I assure you it was with the keenest pleasure and expectation that I received word from Mr. Hammond, present Superintendent of Cushman, that I may again, in the name of the Association and of the school Faculty, invite you to the Seventh Annual Reunion and Conference of the Former Students of the Cushman School, to be held at the school June 17, 1918.

The Reunion this year should be an event of unusual significance. The two years just passed have seen many changes, both in the management and student body of the school. We should feel the necessity of our getting acquainted with the pupils and giving them the encouragement of our interests and support. We should get familiar with the advanced methods of the school that we may see at first hand the possibilities there are for the education of our children. We should keep in close touch with the school that our criticism may be always just and constructive; that our support may be genuine and effective.

The program for the day includes three distinct features that we should come prepared to make the most of.

Let us make the selection of our officers and the new organization of our Association a matter of first importance.

Come prepared to take part in the afternoon conference and to say a word at the evening patriotic meeting. Let us put the best there is in us into the Association and work to make the Association an effective force in the education and uplift of our race. Make your presence in some way a contribution to the interest of your old school. I can assure you it will be a pleasure you will long remember.

Very cordially yours,
V. H. Johnson, President.

Aug. 17 1919

Mr. Hammond,

I received those letters you send me for the children. And I mail half of them to the best boys I know.

I really got two children for cushion. It's a girl about sixteen years old and her brother, Alice Thomas and Jim Thomas. I met their mother and she told me that she want
her girl and son to stay in Cushman for three years and I told her it will be done; so I gave two of these letter and they saw their chance. The only thing they will wait for is their free transportation. and I hope they will get it. besides this I don't know how many boys are going I think Jerry takes two or three with me. but they are not here so I have to wait for them but I will be back before October
I am trying my best to take lot of boys to cushman. They always ask me how good the school is. The only word I could say is good. And I hope you will treat them good if they ever get there. I think they will like to stay if you are good to them. And behind please don't treat them like John Bobsons if they ever get there. This will be all yours.

Sincerely,

Freddy Kaplan
VACATION REQUEST FOR PUPIL.

We, the undersigned, respectfully request that leave of absence be granted

Freddy Nostrum

from June 24, 1919 to Sept. 5th, 1919... to enable him to visit his home. We hereby agree to pay all expenses, including round-trip railroad fares, sleeping-car fares, meals, lodging, stage fares, telegraph and telephone messages, and other incidental expenses connected with the visit. We further agree that said pupil shall be returned to the Cushman school on or before the last day of the leave of absence.

We further agree to deposit with the superintendent of the school sufficient money to defray all expenses to be incurred, and should the pupils, or any one of them, fail to return to school at such time as may be agreed upon by the parents or guardian and the superintendent of the school in which the pupils are enrolled, the amount of money remaining with the superintendent, necessary to the return transportation of the pupil or pupils so neglecting to return, shall be forfeited to the Government; provided, however, that in all cases where it can be shown that there were justifiable reasons for the failure of the return of the pupil the forfeiture of the funds shall not take place.

If the pupil neglecting to return is from a reservation, the superintendent of the reservation shall certify to the reasons for the nonreturn of the pupil or pupils. If a pupil's home is not on a reservation, then the certificate of some reputable, disinterested citizen of the community may be requested as to the reasons for the nonreturn of the pupil or pupils.

EXTRACTS FROM SCHOOL RULES.

If the facilities of a school are not needed as provided in the above paragraph, children showing any Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, and who if debarred from Government schools could not obtain an education, may be enrolled in the day and boarding schools of their reservation; these children, however, may not be transferred to nonreservation schools without special permission from the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

Before enrolling a pupil superintendents must satisfy themselves whether he has left any other school without permission and whether he has been expelled for cause from any other school. A pupil who has been expelled for cause from any Indian school may be reenrolled in any Indian school only with permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

"The Secretary of the Interior may in his discretion withhold rations, clothing, and other annuities from Indian parents or guardians who refuse to or neglect to send or keep their children of proper school age in some school during a reasonable portion of each year." (27 Stat. L., 635. See 2 Dec. Comp., 308.)
