Dear Sir:

I am in receipt of your letter and telegram in which you make inquiry regarding your son, Alec. In reply thereto you are advised that Alec, with two other boys, broke into a store in the vicinity of the Cushman School and stole a number of articles. They were apprehended by the police and were given a fair trial and as a result two of the boys were sentenced to the reform school at Monroe, Washington. Alec received a sentence to the reform school at Chehalis.

Mr. Lehr, teacher at the Chehalis Day School, near Oakville, became much interested in Alec last summer and had Alec with him for a month. He understands Alec thoroughly and has had a knowledge of the several thefts of which Alec has been accused of. Alec made a clean breast of his failings to Mr. Lehr last summer. At that time Mr. Lehr gave Alec some wholesome advice to avoid getting into trouble in the future, but it appears that Alec did not heed the advice. I will just add that Alec was not lead by the other boys to commit this last act. In fact he admits that he was one of the prime leaders in the affair. Mr. Lehr took the matter up with the Juvenile Court in Tacoma and asked that the Court suspend the sentence on Alec and that he, Mr. Lehr, would act as sponsor for Alec if he would be turned over to him. After due consideration, the Juvenile Court suspended Alec's sentence and ordered him to be placed under the personal supervision of Mr. Lehr.

Mr. Lehr is very conscientious and is thoroughly earnest in his desire to give Alec a chance to make good. He has Alec with him now and is doing the right thing by him.
To Remi Heauvais, #2.

I suggest that you write encouraging letters to Alec and at the same time advise him to follow the instructions of Mr. Lehr. Any averse criticism you might make to Alec in your letters will not be beneficial to him. Alec fully realizes that if he fails to make good to Mr. Lehr, that he will be turned over to the Court for commitment to the Chehalis Industrial School. The sentence imposed by the Court is from one to fifteen years, which has been suspended on the conditions above named.

Alec is a bright boy and Mr. Lehr believes that by following out a course of discipline with him, and careful supervision of him, that he can make a good man out of him. From what he can learn from Alec, of his early training, he has not known the care of a mother and but little personal oversight by his father. The influence of a parent over a child either for good or bad is one of the most potent in making either good or bad men.

Very respectfully,

Special Agent in Charge.

EXTRACTS FROM SCHOOL RULES.

If the facilities of a school are not needed as provided in the above paragraph, children showing any Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, and who if debarred from Government schools could not obtain an education, may be enrolled in the day and boarding schools of their reservation; these children, however, may not be transferred to nonreservation schools without special permission from the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

Before enrolling a pupil superintendents must satisfy themselves whether he has left any other school without permission and whether he has been expelled for cause from any other school. A pupil who has been expelled for cause from any Indian school may be reenrolled in any Indian school only with permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

"The Secretary of the Interior may in his discretion withhold rations, clothing, and other annuities from Indian parents or guardians who refuse to or neglect to send or keep their children of proper school age in some school during a reasonable portion of each year." (27 Stat. L., 635. See 2 Dec. Comp., 308.)

RECORD CARD—VOCATIONAL DIVISION.

Each boy should devote three years to the work of this department during the vocational period. When he has accomplished the work as outlined in the course of study in a satisfactory manner, a suitable rating should be recorded. Ratings should be based on a practical application as well as a theoretical knowledge of the principles involved.

Ratings should be excellent (E), good (G), fair (F). If pupil's work is not worthy of a rating of fair, no grade should be recorded. Under "Remarks" mention anything that indicates interest, conduct, and proficiency of the pupil.

This card should be filed in folder for record of pupil (5-197). If the pupil is transferred to another school, a copy of this card should be sent to the officer in charge of the school to which he is transferred.
