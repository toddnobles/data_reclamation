Mr. Oscar H. Keller, Chief Clerk in Charge,

Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Mr. Keller:

Your letter of May 14th relative to the return of Allen Cawker to the Cushman School. Upon the receipt of your letter I called at the Peterson home but Mrs. Peterson was away. Allen met me at the door and I told him that his father had sent money to your office for his return to Arizona. Also asked if he wished to go. He seemed very much pleased and promised me he would be off right away. I had supposed that he had returned to the school until Saturday evening when Mrs. Peterson called upon me saying that Allen wanted to go but that Lucy Andrews had persuaded him not to go, but to come and live with them. Lucy Andrews—Allen is a cousin. Kimball Sherwood is her father and an uncle to the boy. Kimball would rather that the boy would go to his father, but will not take the initiative.

Yours very respectfully,

William C. Miller
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Washington.
June 3, 1920.

Mr. Henry Allen,
Potlatch, Wash.

Dear Sir:

I understand that your wife has used her influence to keep Allen Cawker, who is a cousin, from going to live with his father.

You know that Allen's mother is not able to care for him and has not done so for years. His father is very anxious to have the boy with him as his sister Edith is there. Allen has an allotment in the south and it is legally the place where he should live.

We have money in the office sent by his father to pay his expenses to Arizona. Now that the father has provided a place for the boy, I think it best that he start for the south with as little delay as possible. If the boy does not have any money to pay his way to Cushman, Mr. Miller will advance it, I know, and he can be reimbursed later.

Very respectfully,

Clerk in Charge.

Dear Sir:

Allen Cawker left school yesterday with the Petersons and is probably now on the Skokomish Reservation. His father has sent funds, which reached me yesterday, for the payment of Allen's fare down to Arizona where his father lives.

The father desires to have the boy with him as it appears that his mother, Minnie Cawker, has practically given him no consideration for the last few years. May 3, 1920

Supt. C. Peel  
Tacoma, Wash.

My dear Sir,

This letter in regard to my boy Allen Cawker who is enrolled in your school, my desire is that you please allow him to come to me. I understand that his mother has neglected him for the past 3 years & at present no one knows her whereabouts. I ask you thinking that it's in your power to say what Allen should do since he is under your care, I believe that his mother would not object if she could be found. He has allotment of land here & it's really his home. He has no land there at his mother's home. It is my duty to care for him if his mother neglects him. Last summer Former Supt. H. A. Hammond agree to transfer him to Phoenix Indian School when school will be out this year, so I hope he may have told you about it. I hope to hear from you very soon in regard to this, when I hear from you if he will come, I will wire to you some money to pay his fare & spending money on his way, let me know when he will leave there.

May I mention this that his sister Edith who had been there at your school & later at Fort Lapwai Idaho, hospital came to me last June, Supt Dr. Reid of Fort Lapwai family her to come after he had found out that I was her father & that her mother could not be found, she is now enrolled at the Phoenix Indian School, that is why I do wish to hope that you permit my boy to be transferred there. I will pay all his expenses, & will wire the money as soon as I hear that he can come.

For 14 years I have not seen my boy, Sam a Pima Indian living here on reservation & Sam in a position to take good care of my children, as I say Allen has allotment of 20 acres of farming land & 60 of desert land he will soon be old enough to farm his land. I have been writing to him & he says he wants to come, please help me & permit him to come to us, if you think it best for Alan & come to us, my daughter & I will be very happy if you will allow Allen to come.

I will close & please answer soon.

I remain yours very respectfully,

Harvey A. Cawker

Dept. C.H. Hammond
Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Sir,

This letter is regard to my son Allen Cawker whom I have learning that he is there in your school. My desire is to have my son come to me, if you will permit him to come. Since I find out that he is there, I had written to him & he answered & said he wanted to come to me, so it remains what you say, their mother can not be located for this reason. Capt. H.R. Breid of Lapwai, Idaho has permitted my daughter to come here, it is for the same reason that I am asking you now about my boy, since their mother can't be found it seems right & proper that I as their father should have their care. I am willing to pay his rail-road fare from there to Phoenix, Arizona, so if you grant my request, please let me know the cost of a ticket & I will forward the same.

I have been trying to find out where my children are since they left, but could never get in touch with them, & only recently when my daughter went to the sanatorium at Lapwai, Idaho. Edith is now with me & is getting along nicely, & to be sure both I & my daughter would be very happy if Allen can be permitted to join us here in Arizona.

I have not heard from Allen since July & suppose he is out of school, if he is, please let me know where he is so I can write to him, hope to hear from you soon. I remain yours truly,

Harvey Cawker
Chandler, Arizona.

SPECIAL NOTE.
This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All the blanks must be properly filled in every case.
If the information called for on any part of this blank is not known, that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. Whether the parents are living or dead, their names should be given.
The person who signs the blank in connection with the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

Applicable laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

Compliance must be given to all orders and instructions promulgated in connection with the new course of study issued December 1, 1915.

The information as to public school facilities called for in the certificate of the Superintendent (third page hereof) must be given, and all superintendents are especially directed to exhaust every means to secure enrollment of Indian children in the State public schools, pursuant to instructions heretofore issued in this regard.
