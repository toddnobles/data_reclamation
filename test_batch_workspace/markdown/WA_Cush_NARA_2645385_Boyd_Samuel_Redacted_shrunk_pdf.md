Dear Sir:

Your kind letter of August 13th at hand and in reply wish to say. Samuel is taking treatment from the doctor for the after effects of the serious and well-nigh fatal sicknesses of last term. We do not blame the faculty of Custman for his illness but to the damp climate of the Sound country.

And Mr. Hammond asks in all sincerity if you could not recommend and have the children transferred to some other good school in the interior or in a climate more suited to Samuel's special temperament and state of health. According to his physician he needs special care and climate to avoid long trouble.

Thanking you for your kindness in the past and asking your kind consideration,

I beg to remain,

Sincerely,

Mrs. Maggie Boyd

June 28, 1918.

Mrs. Maggie Boyd  
Route #1 Box 49  
Kettle Falls, Washington.

Dear Mrs. Boyd:

Your letter of the 25th., is at hand. As I wrote you day before yesterday, Sam is getting along all right and is very anxious to return home for the vacation. The other children are well at this date and appear to be enjoying themselves. We try to keep them busy enough to keep them out of mischief but not to overtax them. They have plenty of time for play as well.

Don't worry about your children, Mrs. Boyd. If anything serious happens to them I will advise you promptly. Sam has been ill repeatedly but at only one time did our physician report his condition as critical.

Your other children have had various spells of grippe, measles, mumps and so forth but are to all appearances enjoying themselves. They will, of course, feel downhearted at Sam's leaving them—but we will try to make them happy.

Very sincerely yours

Superintendent

Dear Mrs. Boyd:

    Samuel is again convalescing and I hope will soon be fully recovered.

    He was discharged from the hospital some time ago but after a few days he returned with an attack of pleurisy which later turned into pleura-pneumonia.

May 3 - Returned with the mumps.

May 13 - Dismissed. Lost 7 pounds.

May 24 - Returned with pleura pneumonia and rheumatism. While the pneumonia was in a light form, pus has formed in the right cavity which possibly may have to be drawn off.

June 1 - He is running a much lower temperature than Millard and Dr. Kinnear does not consider him seriously ill.

At Dr. Kinnear's request I am sending you a report of Millard Lang's case and as Samuel Boyd's is similar tho not so serious, I have included his.

Dr. Kinnear tells me that a large percentage of pneumonia cases this year are being followed by the forming of pus as in these cases.

Out at the Base hospital they have many cases all of which are at once drained and which is doubtless the best procedure, but Dr. Kinnear has the parents to consider and he has reason to think that both of these cases are yielding to the treatment and the operation may not be necessary.
