The law provides:
"That no part of this appropriation, or any other appropriation provided for herein except appropriations made pursuant to treaties, shall be used to educate children of less than one-fourth Indian blood whose parents are citizens of the United States and of the State wherein they live and where there are adequate free-school facilities provided and the facilities of the Indian schools are needed for pupils of more than one-fourth Indian blood."

EXTRACTS FROM SCHOOL RULES.

If the facilities of a school are not needed as provided in the above paragraph, children showing any Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, and who if debarred from Government schools could not obtain an education, may be enrolled in the day and boarding schools of their reservation; these children, however, may not be transferred to nonreservation schools without special permission from the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

Before enrolling a pupil superintendents must satisfy themselves whether he has left any other school without permission and whether he has been expelled for cause from any other school. A pupil who has been expelled for cause from any Indian school may be reenrolled in any Indian school only with permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Attention is invited to Circulars Nos. 1039 and 1044, which must be observed.

"The Secretary of the Interior may in his discretion withhold rations, clothing, and other annuities from Indian parents or guardians who refuse or neglect to send or keep their children of proper school age in some school during a reasonable portion of each year." (27 Stat. L., 635. See 2 Dec. Comp., 308.)

DEPARTMENT OF THE INTERIOR
UNITED STATES INDIAN SERVICE

Colville Agency, Nespelem, Wash.,
Nov. 26, 1919.

Mr. E. H. Hammond, Supt.,
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Mr. Hammond:

The application of Louie Martin which you recently forwarded to this office has been referred to the District Farmer for completion. Before returning this application, I desire to advise you regarding Louie's status at this Agency.

Some time last spring, a complaint was made by Father Caldi of St. Mary's Mission, wherein it was alleged that some one, presumably boys, had entered the girls dormitory at night and had disturbed the girls and the sister in charge. I made an investigation and found out who the guilty rascals were. It developed that Louie was one of the party. He was arrested and taken before the Indian court and sentenced to a term in the guard-house. As is customary he was placed on his honor and not locked up as perhaps he should have been. After several days he deserted and went to one of the small reservations in British Columbia. The matter was taken up with Lieut. F. J.C. Ball, Indian Agent, for the Okanogan Reservation and I think it was made so interesting for Louie that he retraced his steps.

Louie's record is not of the best. Shortly before assuming charge of this Agency, while serving a sentence in the Guard-house for some offense, Louie, with some other boys broke into the Matron's quarters where she was caring for a couple of wayward Indian girls. I am writing you regarding this in order that you may be fully advised as to Louie's inclinations.
#2 E. H. H.
11-26-19

If Louie desires to straighten up and make a man of himself, I know that you are willing to help him, as I am.

However, owing to his record I feel that it is best to advise you in order that you may keep an eye on Louie's activities. I may be wrong, but I believe that Louie is looking for a warm berth for the winter, and will return to the "call of the wild" in the spring.

Very truly yours,

Fred O Morgan
Superintendent

Cushman Indian School  
Tacoma, Wash., Dec. 3, 1919

Mr. Fred O. Morgan, Supt.  
Colville Indian Agency  
Nespelem, Washington

Dear Mr. Morgan:

Your letter of November 26th was received. I was somewhat surprised at its contents as Louis Martin has been very quiet and well behaved since his arrival.

I have talked him over with the Disciplinarian and will see that he is carefully watched as long as he is here. I do not intend to retain anyone in school whose conduct is not irreproachable. This year, so far, not a single case of discipline has been brought to my attention and since the first two weeks of school, there has been no case of desertion; and I cannot afford to let Louie start the ball to rolling. I will have a talk with him and if he really wishes a chance to make good, he may have it, with the understanding that he goes, the first misstep he takes.

Very truly yours,

Superintendent.
