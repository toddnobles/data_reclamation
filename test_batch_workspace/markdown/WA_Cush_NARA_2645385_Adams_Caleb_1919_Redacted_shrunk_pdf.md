SPECIAL NOTE.
This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All the blanks must be properly filled in every case.
If the person called for on any part of the blank is not known, that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. Whether the parents are living or dead, their names must be given.
The person who signs the blank as consenting to the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

Applicable laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

Compliance must be given to all orders and instructions promulgated in connection with the new course of study issued December 1, 1915.

The information as to public school facilities called for in the certificate of the Superintendent (third page hereof) must be given, and all superintendents are especially directed to exhaust every means to secure enrollment of Indian children in the State public schools, pursuant to instructions heretofore issued in this regard.

Mr. O. L. Babcock, Supt.
Warmsprings Indian Agency
Warmsprings, Ore.

Dear Mr. Babcock:

Caleb Adams was in the office today and gave me the enclosed letter from his mother. I have exhausted my transportation funds for the current fiscal year and could not pay Caleb's way if I had the money as he has not yet completed his term. As soon as our work around the yard is completed, I shall be in a position to give Caleb leave of absence for the half day to earn some spending money in the country.

I do not know how true the statements in such letters are, but they exert a most depressing influence on the children, making them discontented and homesick. I am forwarding you the letter for your information in case the woman is really in need of assistance.

Very sincerely yours,

Superintendent.

June 27 1919

Mr. E. H. Hammond, Supt.,
Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Mr. Hammond:

I have your letter inclosing the letter from Mrs. Towash relative to Caleb's coming home.

It is true that Mrs. Towash has not been feeling well, but she is living in company with another woman. Should her physical condition become acute I would not hesitate to send for Caleb; but at the present time I can not believe that this reservation offers Caleb any inducements to return, and I am sure that he would be the loser thereby.

If Caleb works through his vacation he will be able to send his mother a little financial aid, and in the meantime our Field Matron is making frequent visits for the purpose of looking after Mrs. Towash.

When Caleb was at home he did not contribute much to the comfort and support of his mother but, as boys of
his age are frequently apt to do, left for the society of the young people of his age, often being gone for days at a time.

Assure Caleb that I will keep him duly informed as to his mother's condition, and that should she become dangerously sick he will have ample notification.

Mrs. Towash has not been confined to her bed, and I am of the opinion that her present run-down condition is due to her grief for the loss of her husband during the epidemic last winter. While I greatly sympathize with her in her physical and mental affliction, I do not believe that she should ask Caleb to return, and would respectfully recommend against it.

Very sincerely yours,

O. L. Babcock
Superintendent.

Superintendent Warm Springs Agency  
Warm Springs,  
Oregon.

My dear Mr. Babcock:

Last week Caleb Adams and Edward Cornoyer disappeared from school about the same time that a number of Pendleton boys, stranded from a Wild West show, left town. The boys, before leaving, informed their friends that they were going out to the country to work, and I spent several days in trying to locate them.

From the letter which I enclose Caleb has evidently reached home and if what says is true, a month on the wood pile would not hurt him any (Suggestion (?)).

At the close of school, I offered the larger boys a chance to earn a dollar a day, and board, to do the inside painting, among them Caleb and Edward; and I advanced to Caleb for Fourth of July spending money $1.

I am sorry that he reached home to trouble you this summer. He would not have done so had not the misleading information coming from a source which I considered reliable.

If he has funds on hand to pay for his own transportation and escort, I would appreciate your returning him. My funds are not yet available.

Wishing you a pleasant and productive vacation, I am

Sincerely yours,

Superintendent.
