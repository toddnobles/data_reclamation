Cushman Indian School  
Tacoma, Wash., Dec. 24, 1919

Eugene W. Hill, Supt. & Physician  
Taholah Indian Agency  
Taholah, Wash.

Dear Doctor:

I have given vacation leave to Catherine Hawk, Lizzie Moses, and Hattie Major, as requested by you. The last two have left deposits of $4.04 each for their return fare.

I have advised Mrs. Cole that she may keep her daughter at home provided she refunds to the School tuition and board for the three months Catharine has been here, at the rate of $20.00 per month. Catherine's conduct has been irreproachable since her admission to Cushman and I have only Doctor Benson's statement of Mrs. Cole's complaint that Mr. Cole and Catherine were found in a compromising position by the wife who voluntarily requested Catherine's enrollment in this school.

Hattie Major and Lizzie Moses have been two of our nice girls—but a letter or so from men on the reservation to the girls, particularly to Hattie, show that the men are not to blame that the girls are not spoiled.

Simon Charley has no children at Cushman. His brother's children are enrolled, however, by Simon Charley. He has been in the habit of asking for them, under promise to return them to school promptly, but with no intention of returning them, unless forced to do so.

If Simon Charley is going to remove to Taholah and if his children can attend the Taholah school, I have no objection to canceling their enrollments here, provided Simon Charley is willing to refund to the school tuition and board at the rate of $20.00 a month for each child since their admission here, December 2, 1918.

With best wishes for a Merry Christmas and a very Happy, prosperous New Year, I am

Sincerely yours,
Subject,
Josephine Charlie,

Mr. E H Hammond,

Washington,

Dear Sir,

Mr Simon Charlie of this place informs me that he has a daughter at the school Josephine Charlie that he desires to remove and place in school here, stating that the mother now in Hoquiam is to come here and live in a house they own. If there is no objection from your office I will approve such action. If there is anything that militates against it please advise me of same.

Respectfully,

Eugene W. McClellan
Supt, & Phys,

TAHOLAH INDIAN AGENCY,
Taholah, Washington, October 3, 1919.

Mr. Simon Charley,
Hoquiam, Washington.

Dear Sir:

In reply to your appeal for the services of Alice Charley's little boy Joe, who should be in school, you are informed that I do not favor your plan of keeping this child from returning to the Cushman School. I am sorry if you need him to run errands for you, which is about all that a child of his age can do, but you should think of his future.

I wrote Supt. Hammond, of the Cushman School, that Alice's children should be returned, and I hope he has arranged for them accordingly, and if you have retained Joe, I hope he will let me know.

Very truly yours,

Supt. & Physician.

OOB

TAHOLAH INDIAN AGENCY,
Taholah, Washington, January 21, 1919.

Mr. Simon Charley,
Tacoma, Washington.

Dear Sir:

Replying to your letter in which you request me to intercede with Supt. E. H. Hammond for the release of your children or step-children from the Cushman School, you are informed that I know of no reason why they should not remain there.

One trouble with the attitude of the Indians toward the schools is that they want to be taking the children out of one school and putting them in another in the course of the school term, or keeping them out until the police are sent to put them in school.

I commend Supt. Hammond for refusing to have the children interrupted in their work at this time.

Very truly yours,

Otis L. Pease

OOB/MB
Supt. & Physician.

Cushman Indian School,
Tacoma, Wash., Feb. 1, 1919.

Dr. Otis O. Benson,
Supt. Taholah Indian Agency,
Taholah, Washington.

My Dear Doctor:

The carbon copy of your letter to Mr. Simon Charley was duly received. I appreciate very much your attitude in the case.

When the mother came for the children, the latter had just recovered from the chicken pox which they introduced to Cushman. The mother claimed her little ones were ragged, etc., etc., in spite of the evidence before her. I feel sure that after a few requests to permit the children to drift in and out of school during the term are turned down, we shall be able to put a stop in a great measure, to this pernicious habit.

I regretted very much not being able to send an employee to Taholah for children at the opening of school because Cushman is the logical school for your children—but as a matter of fact every suitable employee was already out on the road, and there was no one to send.

If you have a few more pupils that should be in school, please let me know when and where to send for them, and I will do so.

Very sincerely yours,

Superintendent

SPECIAL NOTE.

This form must be executed in duplicate when a child is transferred from a reservation to a nonreservation school. The Superintendent of the nonreservation school will retain the original for his files, and the duplicate shall be deposited in the reservation school records. The reservation superintendent should send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate as provided by law. All the blanks must be properly filled in every instance. If the information called for on any part of the blank is not known, that fact should be stated. No space should be left unfilled. In filling out the parent's name, both names must be given. The person who signs the blank as consenting to the transfer should indicate his relation to the applicant by marking out the word "parent," "guardian," or "next of kin," leaving unmarked only the title appropriate to the signer.
INDORSEMENTS.

Applicable laws relating to the transfer of Indian children from reservations and schools are as follows:

That hereafter no Indian child shall be sent from any Indian reservation to a school beyond the State or Territory in which said reservation is situated without the voluntary consent of the father or mother of such child if either of them are living, and if neither of them are living without the voluntary consent of the next of kin of such child. Such consent shall be made before the agent of the reservation, and he shall send to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs his certificate that such consent has been voluntarily given before such child shall be removed from such reservation. And it shall be unlawful for any Indian agent or other employee of the Government to induce, or seek to induce, by withholding rations or by other improper means, the parents or next of kin of any Indian to consent to the removal of any Indian child beyond the limits of any reservation. (28 Stats., p. 906.)

Provided, That hereafter no Indian child shall be taken from any school in any State or Territory to a school in any other State against its will or without the written consent of its parents. (29 Stats., p. 348.)

That no Indian pupil under the age of fourteen years shall be transported at Government expense to any Indian school beyond the limits of the State or Territory in which the parents of such child reside or of the adjoining State or Territory. (35 Stat. L., 781.)

The rules provide that—

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

An Indian boy or girl 18 years old and over may, without the consent of parents or others, personally sign the application form on its being changed to suit the case; but in all cases where the parents are living they should first be consulted.

Compliance must be given to all orders and instructions promulgated in connection with the new course of study issued December 1, 1915.

The information as to public school facilities called for in the certificate of the Superintendent (third page hereof) must be given, and all superintendents are especially directed to exhaust every means to secure enrollment of Indian children in the State public schools, pursuant to instructions heretofore issued in this regard.

EXTRACT FROM SCHOOL RULES.

If the facilities of a school are not needed as provided in the above paragraph, children showing any Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, and who if debarred from Government schools could not obtain an education, may be enrolled in the day and boarding schools of their reservation; these children, however, may not be transferred to nonreservation schools without special permission from the Commissioner of Indian Affairs.

Before enrolling a pupil superintendents must satisfy themselves whether he has left any other school without permission and whether he has been expelled for cause from any other school. A pupil who has been expelled for cause from any Indian school may be reenrolled in any Indian school only with permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Attention is invited to Circulars Nos. 1039 and 1044, which must be observed.

Indian children whose parents are amply able to pay for their education and who have public school facilities at or near their homes shall not hereafter be enrolled in Government Indian Schools supported by gratuity appropriations, except on payment of actual per capita cost and transportation. (Sec. 6, Declaration of Policy, dated April 17, 1917.)
