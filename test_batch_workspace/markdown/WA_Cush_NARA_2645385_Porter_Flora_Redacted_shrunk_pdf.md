Cushman Indian School
Tacoma, Wash., Aug. 29, 1919

County Superintendent of Public Schools
Pierce County
Tacoma, Washington

My dear Mr. Cox:

Mrs. Hattie Cowden, 612 South Tacoma Avenue, has applied for the discharge of Flora Porter, a [redacted] breed Eskimo girl, who was placed in this school seven years ago by Mrs. Cowden.

Mrs. Cowden informs me that she is a teacher in the Tacoma schools and is acquainted with you. Before granting discharge to the girl, I would like some assurance that Mrs. Cowden is in a position to look after the girl's material welfare and is in other respects able to give a desirable home and protection to this girl.

Thanking you in advance for any information you may be able to give me regarding Mrs. Cowden's financial standing and her character, I am

Very truly yours,

Superintendent.

EHH:MC
Cushman Indian School  
Tacoma, Wash., Sept. 10, 1919

Mrs. Hattie Cowden  
612 So. Tacoma Ave.  
Tacoma, Wash.

Dear Mrs. Cowden:

The references received from the County Superintendent of Schools is satisfactory.

I have a request from a respectable family in Seattle for a young girl. This request is in the shape of an offer to keep the young girl in school until she is through the 8th grade, and to pay her a weekly salary of $5.00. Flora tells me, however, that she does not wish to leave school if she can be enrolled in the Nursing course.

I have no objection whatever to her leaving, except that I know Flora will have a much better future open to her on leaving Cushman #2 as a teacher or assistant nurse in the Alaskan School Service. The Commissioner of Indian Affairs  
Washington, D.C.

My dear Mr. Commissioner:

Nine years ago Mrs. Hattie Cowden enrolled at Cushman School a [blacked-out] blood Eskimo girl, Flora Porter, an orphan, born 1902, for a term of five years, definitely surrendering any claims she might possess on the girl. At the close of the enrollment period Mrs. Cowden did not come for the girl. Two years later, last September, she came for Flora, stating that she needed her assistance in the house and agreed to keep Flora in school and to give her an occasional five dollars for clothing, etc, but of course could not pay her stated wages.

I looked up Mrs. Cowden's references and found them satisfactory, but in the mean time a Mrs. DeCou of Seattle had applied for the girl, promising to keep the girl in school until she had finished the 8th grade and to pay her five dollars a week and her board. This offer was given to Mrs. Cowden who refused it as being most unsatisfactory and insisted on having Flora Porter turned over to her. This I am very much disinclined to do without explicit instructions from you as Flora is an orphan and I believe under the regulations, as representative of the Secretary of the Interior, I am her legal guardian.

In two years time Flora will have completed the course at Cushman and will be in a position to obtain appointment in the Alaskan Teaching Service at $75.00 a month. Last June one of our Eskimo girls received appointment as Assistant Nurse in the Aklak, Alaska, hospital at $70.00 a month; and Flora can do the same which I think is a much better prospect for her than the one offered either by Mrs. Cowden or Mrs. DeCou.

I am writing to you thus in detail as Mrs. Cowden advised me that she had taken the matter up with you.

Respectfully,

Superintendent.

INDORSEMENT.

A child showing one-sixteenth or less Indian blood, whose parents live on an Indian reservation, Indian fashion, who, if debarred from the Government schools, could not obtain an education, may be permitted in the reservation day and boarding schools, but it is preferable that it be not transferred to a nonreservation school, without special permission from the Office. Children showing one-eighth or less Indian blood, whose parents do not live on an Indian reservation, whose home is among white people where there are churches and schools, who are presumed to have adopted the white man's manners and customs, and are to all intents and purposes white people, are debarred from enrollment in the Government nonreservation and reservation schools. Superintendents, in all cases where doubt exists as to the degree of Indian blood of a child proposed for transfer, should fully satisfy themselves of the facts by affidavits from reliable persons, which affidavits must be kept on file at the school.

A pupil who has been regularly enrolled in a nonreservation school must not be taken to any other nonreservation school without the consent of both Superintendents and the Commissioner of Indian Affairs, and Superintendents will be held to strict accountability for such pupils taken to their schools.

A pupil dismissed from school for cause must not be enrolled in any other school without the permission of the Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Full facts must be submitted with each request.
